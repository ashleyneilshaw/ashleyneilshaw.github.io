Research

The following is an overview of my research interests.

The Nature and Content of Desire

I am interested in questions concerning the nature and content of desire. What is the relationship between desires and phenomena like pleasure, affect, reward, need and representations of value? Are all desires propositional attitudes? What is the nature of appetitive states?

Reasons and Rationality

I am interested generally in questions about the relationship between reasons and rationality. I am also interested in the role desires play in rational agency. My PhD thesis develops a framework for understanding how our conative system comprises a capacity for non-deliberative responsiveness to a class of normative reasons for action.

The Metaphysics of Need

Many have plausibly thought that claims of need are morally and politically significant. Recently, I have been working on a foundational question concerning the metaphysics of need. What are needs? How are needs related to goals, desires and functions? I think that reflecting on need can elucidate questions about the function and nature of desire.

Other Interests

I am working in other areas like the philosophy of language and epistemology. I have worked on the semantics of desire ascription and I am co-authoring a paper with Poppy Mankowitz on the pragmatics of pejorative language. I have also worked on self-knowledge where I am interested in the role of consciousness in our ability self-ascribe our mental states.


Papers

"Desire and Satisfaction" (forthcoming) The Philosophical Quarterly, doi: 10.1093/pq/pqz068. Abstract | Final Draft
Desire satisfaction has not received detailed philosophical examination. Yet intuitive judgments about the satisfaction of desires have been used as data points guiding theories of desire, desire content and the semantics of 'desire'. This paper examines desire satisfaction and the standard propositional view of desire. First, I argue that there are several distinct concepts of satisfaction. Second, I argue that separating them defuses a difficulty for the standard view in accommodating desires that Derek Parfit described as "implicitly conditional on their own persistence", a problem posed by Shieva Kleinschmidt, Kris McDaniel and Ben Bradley. The solution undercuts a key motivation for rejecting the standard view in favour of more radical accounts proposed in the literature.
