About

Hi, I’m Ashley Shaw. I am a PhD candidate and Postgraduate Teaching Assistant in the UCL Philosophy Department. I started my PhD in 2014. My research is supported by the AHRC London Arts and Humanities Partnership. Before starting my PhD, I completed the MPhil Stud. programme at UCL.

Research

Core Research

I work primarily in the Philosophy of Mind, Action and Philosophical Psychology; more specifically, I am interested in issues like the nature and content of desire, the relationship between rational agency and acting for reasons, as well as the role of desire in psychological explanations of action. My most recent research project has been my Ph.D thesis (“Desire and Practical Rationality”) which concerns the role of desire in practical rationality. I develop a framework for understanding how our conative system comprises a capacity for non-deliberative responsiveness to a class of normative reasons for action. The account emphasises the biological function of our desires and the conative system to which they belong.

Additional Interests

I am working in other areas like the philosophy of language and epistemology, especially where these areas intersect with my main research interest. I have worked on the semantics of desire ascription. I am currently co-authoring a paper with Poppy Mankowitz on the pragmatics of pejorative language. I have also worked on doxastic self-knowledge. In my M.Phil thesis, I defended a ‘transparency’ account of self-knowledge, developing a framework due to Christopher Peacocke for understanding consciousness-based epistemic reasons for the self-ascription of beliefs. I am currently also thinking about knowledge of our desires.

Papers

"Desire and Satisfaction" (forthcoming) The Philosophical Quarterly, doi: 10.1093/pq/pqz068.
Abstract | Final Draft
Desire satisfaction has not received detailed philosophical examination. Yet intuitive judgments about the satisfaction of desires have been used as data points guiding theories of desire, desire content and the semantics of `desire'. This paper examines desire satisfaction and the standard propositional view of desire. First, I argue that there are several distinct concepts of satisfaction. Second, I argue that separating them defuses a difficulty for the standard view in accommodating desires that Derek Parfit described as "implicitly conditional on their own persistence", a problem posed by Shieva Kleinschmidt, Kris McDaniel and Ben Bradley. The solution undercuts a key motivation for rejecting the standard view in favour of more radical accounts proposed in the literature.
